The article examines a pivotal [study](https://doi.org/10.1093/sleep/26.2.117) from the University of Pennsylvania and Washington State University that challenges the common belief that six hours of sleep is “enough.”

Researchers tracked participants over two weeks, limiting them to four, six, or eight hours of sleep, and compared them to a group deprived of sleep for two full days.

They found that <mark>individuals sleeping six hours per night exhibit the same mental deficits as those who have been awake for two days straight</mark>.

Most importantly, the study reveals a dangerous “self-perception gap”: <mark>participants getting six hours didn’t feel sleep-deprived even as their performance plummeted. The brain loses the ability to monitor its own impairment.</mark>

---

1. Adaptation is an Illusion: We think we “adjust” to less sleep, but in reality, we only lose our ability to notice our own decline. The belief that six hours of sleep is sufficient for high performance is a scientifically proven fallacy.
2. Performance vs Feeling: There is a decoupling between **how tired we feel** and **how well we actually function**. Feeling “fine” is not a reliable metric for productivity or safety.
3. The Tipping Point: Performance doesn’t just dip and level off; it continues to degrade linearly as the sleep debt increases.
